## Purpose and scope

This document is the judge-facing provenance record for source cards `S01`–`S10` in [`lib/case-data.mjs`](./lib/case-data.mjs). It identifies the historical source behind each card, explains every material shortening or rewrite, and states the inference and reuse limits that apply to the card.

The case-data module contains short excerpts, original SourceCourt summaries, and outbound links. It does not bundle the full historical works, archive scans, Snow's map image, a death-level dataset, or modern personal data.

This is an educational provenance assessment, not legal advice. Public-domain status and archive terms can vary by jurisdiction and by the particular scan, transcription, or webpage being reused.

## S01 — Deaths clustered around the pump

- **Source:** John Snow, *On the Mode of Communication of Cholera*, second edition (1855), Map 1 and its caption.
- **Link used by the case:** [MSU John Snow Archive, book and map record](https://johnsnow.matrix.msu.edu/work.php/id=15-78-52/).
- **Displayed excerpt:** “A black mark or bar for each death is placed in the situation of the house.”
- **How SourceCourt edited it:** This is the verbatim opening of the caption sentence. The qualifying ending—identifying the house as the place where the fatal attack occurred—was omitted, and a period was supplied. The card title, summary, limitation, facets, and stance are SourceCourt editorial text rather than Snow's labels.
- **Permitted use:** Evidence that Snow's published map encoded deaths at house locations and showed a spatial cluster near Broad Street.
- **Boundary:** The marks represent recorded deaths at houses, not every infection, an individual's exposure location, or laboratory-confirmed cholera. Spatial clustering alone does not establish that pump water caused the deaths or exclude shared neighborhood factors.
- **Rights note:** Snow's 1855 text and map are public-domain historical material in the United States. The MSU Archive's modern transcription, annotations, metadata, and site presentation may have separate protection; link and credit the archive, and do not copy its editorial material or page design without permission.

## S02 — Families reported using Broad Street water

- **Source:** John Snow, “The Cholera near Golden Square, and at Deptford,” *Medical Times and Gazette* (23 September 1854).
- **Link used by the case:** [MSU John Snow Archive, contemporary report](https://johnsnow.matrix.msu.edu/documentUploads/15-78-2CE/15-78-2CE-22-Doc19-JS-CholGolSq1854-09-23.pdf).
- **Displayed excerpt:** “I was informed that the deceased persons used to drink the pump water from Broad Street, either constantly or occasionally.”
- **How SourceCourt edited it:** This is a continuous clause from Snow's contemporary report, beginning after his count of 61 instances. Typeset line-break hyphenation was removed, the initial “I” was retained, and a period was supplied. The surrounding report says exposure was unknown for six deaths and reportedly absent for six, which the card limitation preserves.
- **Permitted use:** Evidence that Snow conducted household inquiries and found a strong reported association between fatal cases and Broad Street pump-water use.
- **Boundary:** The inquiry was retrospective, incomplete, and based on reported behavior. It did not provide a randomized comparison or a complete non-case denominator, and it cannot by itself prove causation or establish exposure for every victim.
- **Rights note:** The underlying 1854 report is public-domain historical text in the United States. The card summary and limitation are SourceCourt editorial text. The MSU-hosted transcription and presentation may include protected modern work and should be credited rather than copied wholesale.

## S03 — The handle was removed after the peak

- **Sources:** Snow's 23 September 1854 report, with fuller discussion in *On the Mode of Communication of Cholera* (1855).
- **Links:** [Current case link: MSU contemporary report](https://johnsnow.matrix.msu.edu/documentUploads/15-78-2CE/15-78-2CE-22-Doc19-JS-CholGolSq1854-09-23.pdf); [full 1855 book text at Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/72894/72894-h/72894-h.htm).
- **Displayed excerpt:** “The number of attacks of cholera had been diminished before this measure was adopted.”
- **How SourceCourt edited it:** This is a continuous sentence from the linked September 1854 report. Typeset line-break hyphenation was removed. The next clause explains that Snow could not determine how much of the decline was due to residents leaving, so the card treats the sentence as a chronology limit rather than proof of an alternative cause.
- **Permitted use:** Evidence that the pump handle was removed on 8 September 1854 after the outbreak had already begun to decline, and that Snow himself acknowledged this timing.
- **Boundary:** The chronology prevents a clean “handle removal immediately ended the outbreak” inference. It also does not show that removal was useless: preventing continued or renewed exposure remains a plausible protective effect, but it is counterfactual rather than directly observed.
- **Rights note:** Both nineteenth-century underlying texts are public domain in the United States. Project Gutenberg distributes a U.S. public-domain transcription; users elsewhere must check local law, and Gutenberg's trademark and framing are not themselves public domain. Modern archive apparatus should not be reproduced as historical source text.

## S04 — The workhouse had its own well

- **Sources:** John Snow, report to the St. James's Cholera Inquiry Committee (12 December 1854; published 1855), with parallel wording in his 1855 book.
- **Links:** [Current case link: MSU committee-report transcription](https://johnsnow.matrix.msu.edu/documentUploads/15-78-2BD/15-78-2BD-22-Doc26-IV-RprtToCIC.pdf); [1855 book at Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/72894/72894-h/72894-h.htm).
- **Displayed excerpt:** “The workhouse has a pump-well on the premises ... and the inmates never sent to Broad Street for water.”
- **How SourceCourt edited it:** The displayed wording follows Snow's 1855 book and omits the intervening clause that the workhouse also received Grand Junction Water Works water. The linked committee report gives the same substantive account but uses the shorter term “pump” rather than “pump-well.” The summary's population figure and outcome count are an editorial synthesis of the report.
- **Permitted use:** A natural comparison: a large institution near the outbreak had an alternative water supply, did not normally fetch Broad Street water, and experienced comparatively few deaths.
- **Boundary:** This is an observational comparison, not a controlled trial. Workhouse residents differed from neighbors in housing, supervision, food, age mix, mobility, and other exposures; the card supports triangulation, not a standalone causal estimate.
- **Rights note:** The underlying report and book are public-domain historical texts in the United States. MSU and Project Gutenberg may retain rights in modern editorial contributions, formatting, metadata, trademarks, or site presentation.

## S05 — Brewery workers largely escaped

- **Sources:** Snow's 1854 committee report and 1855 book, describing the brewery operated by Mr. Huggins.
- **Links:** [Current case link: MSU committee-report transcription](https://johnsnow.matrix.msu.edu/documentUploads/15-78-2BD/15-78-2BD-22-Doc26-IV-RprtToCIC.pdf); [1855 book at Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/72894/72894-h/72894-h.htm).
- **Displayed excerpt:** “The men are allowed a certain quantity of malt liquor, and Mr. Huggins believes they do not drink water at all.”
- **How SourceCourt edited it:** The words are a verbatim opening clause in the source; the original semicolon and continuing sentence were replaced with a period. “Brewery workers largely escaped” is SourceCourt's compact title, not Snow's heading, and “believes” is intentionally retained to preserve that the water-avoidance claim was reported rather than directly measured.
- **Permitted use:** A second natural comparison consistent with reduced exposure to Broad Street water among nearby workers.
- **Boundary:** The employer's belief is not an individual exposure census, and malt liquor, a private well, occupation, time at work, and workforce characteristics are entangled. The comparison cannot alone quantify a protective effect or prove universal non-use of water.
- **Rights note:** The underlying nineteenth-century text is public domain in the United States. The same archive and transcription caveats described for S04 apply.

## S06 — A distant resident still drank the water

- **Source:** John Snow, *On the Mode of Communication of Cholera*, second edition (1855), account of a fatal case in Hampstead whose household obtained Broad Street water.
- **Link used by the case:** [Project Gutenberg, full 1855 text](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/72894/72894-h/72894-h.htm).
- **Displayed excerpt:** “The water was taken on Thursday, 31st August, and she drank of it in the evening, and also on Friday.”
- **How SourceCourt edited it:** This is a continuous sentence from Snow's account. No words were added or removed. The preceding sentence supplies the reported daily cart journey and the resident's preference; the card summary paraphrases that surrounding context rather than placing it inside quotation marks.
- **Permitted use:** An investigated outlier showing why actual water-use histories mattered in addition to residential distance from the pump.
- **Boundary:** One memorable case cannot establish a population-level causal relationship. The exposure account was reported retrospectively, and the card should not be used to identify, sensationalize, or assign blame to the victim or household.
- **Rights note:** Snow's underlying 1855 text is public domain in the United States. The displayed paraphrase is SourceCourt editorial text. Project Gutenberg's jurisdiction, trademark, and presentation caveats apply.

## S07 — The central inquiry favored a diffused agent

- **Source:** Great Britain General Board of Health, Medical Council, *Report of the Committee for Scientific Inquiries in relation to the Cholera-Epidemic of 1854* (1855).
- **Links:** [Current case link: Wellcome Collection record and scan](https://wellcomecollection.org/works/ckquck57); [related report extract at the MSU John Snow Archive](https://johnsnow.matrix.msu.edu/documentUploads/15-78-2B0/15-78-2B0-22-Doc31-GBoH-MedCncl.pdf).
- **Displayed excerpt:** “All point to some atmospheric or other widely diffused agent still to be discovered.”
- **How SourceCourt edited it:** This is a verbatim clause. The omitted beginning identifies the outbreak's sudden onset, immediate climax, and short duration as the observations that “all point” to the proposed agent. SourceCourt capitalized the first word and supplied terminal punctuation.
- **Permitted use:** Evidence that a contemporary central medical inquiry favored an atmospheric or otherwise widely diffused explanation, despite recognizing that the well water was impure. It documents genuine historical disagreement.
- **Boundary:** The card proves that the institution advanced this interpretation; it does not make the interpretation scientifically correct. The inquiry began after the peak, used limited individual exposure evidence, and its inability to identify a pathogen with contemporary methods is not proof that no waterborne pathogen was present.
- **Rights note:** Wellcome marks the underlying historical work as public-domain material. Credit Wellcome Collection and follow its terms before reusing scan files or high-resolution media. Wellcome's record, digitization, metadata, and website are not automatically covered merely because the original report is public domain.

## S08 — Whitehead independently tested the link

- **Source:** Henry Whitehead, *Report of his Special Investigation of Broad Street* (investigation dated 5 June 1855; published with the parish inquiry in July 1855).
- **Link used by the case:** [MSU John Snow Archive, complete report](https://johnsnow.matrix.msu.edu/work.php/id=15-78-7D/).
- **Displayed excerpt:** “The statistics which I shall lay before the Committee are the result of personal inquiry.”
- **How SourceCourt edited it:** The displayed sentence is verbatim. The card title and summary are an editorial synthesis of Whitehead's independent interviews and later support after initial skepticism; the 1854–1867 range also reflects his later retrospective writing rather than only the linked 1855 report.
- **Permitted use:** Evidence that Whitehead gathered an independent, interview-based body of exposure information that supported the connection between pump-water use and illness and helped investigate a plausible contamination route.
- **Boundary:** Whitehead obtained information for only part of the local population, months after the event; selection and recall bias remain possible. His work did not laboratory-confirm the organism, prove the proposed infant was an index case, or establish a single certain contamination event.
- **Rights note:** Whitehead's 1855 report is public-domain historical text in the United States. MSU's modern annotations, transcription choices, metadata, and presentation may be separately protected and should be credited rather than republished wholesale.

## S09 — Water-company comparison across London

- **Source:** John Snow, *On the Mode of Communication of Cholera*, second edition (1855), discussion of the South London water-company comparison.
- **Link used by the case:** [Project Gutenberg, full 1855 text](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/72894/72894-h/72894-h.htm).
- **Displayed excerpt:** “It is obvious that no experiment could have been devised which would more thoroughly test the effect of water supply on the progress of cholera…”
- **How SourceCourt edited it:** This is a continuous clause from Snow's longer sentence, with the initial “it” capitalized because the excerpt begins after a comma. The visible ellipsis marks the omitted continuation, which describes circumstances as placing the experiment ready-made before the observer.
- **Permitted use:** Broader corroboration for Snow's waterborne-transmission theory through an observational natural experiment involving differently supplied South London populations.
- **Boundary:** This comparison is not direct evidence about the Broad Street well, and water-company assignment was not randomized. Population composition, geography, service boundaries, record linkage, and other confounders limit modern causal interpretation.
- **Rights note:** Snow's underlying 1855 text is public domain in the United States; the shortened wording is SourceCourt editorial text. Project Gutenberg's local-law, trademark, and presentation caveats apply.

## S10 — The outbreak was already in decline

- **Source:** Henry Whitehead, “Remarks on the Outbreak of Cholera in Broad Street, Golden Square, London, in 1854,” read to the Epidemiological Society of London on 6 May 1867 and published in its *Transactions* in 1869.
- **Links:** [Current case link: PubMed Central scan/republication](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5523601/); [modern bibliographic record and reprint at Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/ije/article-abstract/43/2/597/679678).
- **Displayed excerpt:** “The outbreak had already reached its climax, and had been steadily on the decline.”
- **How SourceCourt edited it:** This is a verbatim clause with initial capitalization and terminal punctuation supplied. The source continues that the decline had lasted several days before the handle was removed. The card summary's statement that removal might still have prevented renewed exposure paraphrases Whitehead's later counterfactual discussion; it is not an observed outcome.
- **Permitted use:** Evidence from Whitehead's daily fatal-attack chronology against the popular claim that removing the handle immediately caused the outbreak curve to fall.
- **Boundary:** Whitehead wrote thirteen years after the event. His chronology does not show that intervention had no preventive value, while his proposed prevention of a renewed outbreak cannot be treated as certain. “Fatal attacks,” deaths, onset dates, and reported cases should not be used interchangeably.
- **Rights note:** The underlying 1869 article is public-domain historical text in the United States. The PMC record displays a [CC BY-NC-ND 4.0](https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-nc-nd/4.0/) notice for the hosted version, which may include modern editorial or republication elements. SourceCourt uses only a short historical clause and a link; commercial reuse of the hosted PDF, page, scan, or modern editorial material should be cleared under the repository/publisher terms.

## Cross-cutting use and rights rules

1. **SourceCourt's editorial layer is distinguishable from the sources.** Card titles, summaries, dates formatted for the interface, evidence facets, stance labels, limitations, and “supports/challenges” roles are original educational editorial content. They are not historical titles, archival metadata, or claims that the historical author used those categories.
2. **A stance label is pedagogical, not a truth label.** “Supports” means a source card can be used in favor of a case proposition; “challenges” means it can test or qualify one. Neither label certifies the proposition as true or false.
3. **Short excerpts do not replace source reading.** The cards deliberately omit context. Judges and downstream users should follow the links and check the surrounding passage before using a card in scholarship, publication, or legal/historical argument.
4. **Public-domain text is not the same as a public-domain webpage.** The Victorian source works are old enough to be public domain in the United States, but archive annotations, transcriptions, metadata, digitization choices, scan files, branding, and page designs can carry separate rights or contractual conditions.
5. **No archive endorsement is implied.** MSU John Snow Archive, Project Gutenberg, Wellcome Collection, PubMed Central, and Oxford Academic are credited as access points; none is represented as sponsoring or endorsing SourceCourt.
6. **Historical uncertainty must remain visible.** The cards should not be used to claim that Snow isolated *Vibrio cholerae*, that the map alone proved causation, that every victim drank pump water, that one household was conclusively responsible, or that handle removal single-handedly ended the outbreak.
7. **Local-law review remains the reuser's responsibility.** Project Gutenberg's public-domain determination is U.S.-focused, and copyright duration differs across countries. Anyone distributing source text, scans, or media outside this educational project should assess the relevant jurisdiction and the host's current terms.
